## Task 5: Review and Respond to Messages

### Steps:
1. Consolidate all communication channels in one view
2. Check email inbox and sort by priority/urgency
3. Review messaging platform (Slack, Teams, etc.) for unread messages
4. Check LinkedIn messages (covered in Task 4)
5. Review any SMS or other communication channels
6. Categorize messages: urgent, important, routine, FYI
7. Respond to all urgent messages immediately
8. Draft responses to important messages
9. Schedule time blocks for routine responses
10. Archive or file completed conversations
11. Set reminders for follow-ups needed
12. Update task list if messages create new action items

### Resources and Links:
- Email client
- Team messaging platform
- Communication response templates
- Priority matrix for message triage
- Calendar for scheduling follow-ups

### Instructions:

Start by checking all communication channels and creating a master list of unread/pending messages with priority levels. Use the Eisenhower Matrix to categorize: Urgent & Important (respond immediately), Important but Not Urgent (schedule response within 24 hours), Urgent but Not Important (delegate or quick response), Neither Urgent nor Important (batch process or archive). For urgent messages requiring immediate attention, respond within 30 minutes with either a full answer or acknowledgment that you're working on it with expected response time. For important messages, draft thoughtful, complete responses that address all questions or concerns raised. Use templates for common inquiries to save time, but personalize each message. Keep responses clear, concise, and action-oriented with specific next steps or timelines. If a message requires significant time to address properly, send a quick acknowledgment and schedule dedicated time to provide a comprehensive response. Set up email filters and labels to automatically organize incoming messages. Use the two-minute rule: if a response takes less than two minutes, do it immediately rather than adding to your task list. For messages that generate new tasks, immediately add them to your action item list with appropriate priority and deadline. End the day with inbox zero or a clearly organized inbox where all messages are categorized and have scheduled response times.

## Task 7: Rewrite Templates for Old Connections

### Steps:
1. Review current templates being used for old connections
2. Analyze response rates and effectiveness of existing templates
3. Research best practices for re-engagement messaging
4. Identify different categories of old connections (dormant clients, past colleagues, inactive students, etc.)
5. Create customized templates for each category
6. Incorporate personalization variables
7. Draft subject lines that increase open rates
8. Write compelling body content that provides value
9. Include clear call-to-action
10. Test templates with small sample group
11. Gather feedback and refine
12. Create template library with usage guidelines
13. Document when and how to use each template

### Resources and Links:
- Current template repository
- Analytics on previous outreach campaign performance
- Email marketing best practices guides
- CRM system for connection history
- A/B testing results from previous campaigns
- Professional copywriting resources

### Instructions:

Begin by auditing all existing templates used for reconnecting with old connections. Analyze their performance metrics including open rates, response rates, and conversion rates to identify what's working and what isn't. Review the language, tone, length, and structure of current templates. Research current best practices for re-engagement communication, focusing on personalization, value-driven content, and authentic relationship building. Segment your old connections into meaningful categories such as: former clients/customers, past professional colleagues, dormant network connections, alumni, inactive students or program participants, and cold leads who showed initial interest. For each category, create 2-3 template variations that can be personalized based on specific circumstances. Key elements for effective reconnection templates include: personalized opening that references specific shared history or connection point, acknowledgment of time passed without being apologetic, genuine reason for reaching out (not just "checking in"), value proposition or offering (helpful resource, relevant opportunity, interesting information), open-ended question to encourage response, and clear but low-pressure call-to-action. Subject lines should be concise, personal, and intriguing without being clickbait (examples: "Quick question about [specific topic]", "Thought of you when I saw [relevant news]", "[Shared connection] suggested I reach out"). Keep email body concise (150-250 words maximum), focus on the recipient's interests rather than your agenda, and make it easy to respond with a simple yes/no or short answer question. Include personalization variables such as [First Name], [Company], [Last Interaction Date], [Shared Interest/Project], [Mutual Connection] that must be filled in for each message. Create usage guidelines that specify which template to use in which scenario, how much to customize beyond the template, optimal timing for sending, and appropriate follow-up sequences. Test new templates with a small sample of each connection category, measure results, gather any direct feedback, and refine based on data. Organize final templates in an easily accessible library with clear labeling and instructions for use.

9. Create updated schedule charts or calendars
10. Send individual notifications to affected employees
11. Update centralized schedule database or calendar
12. Communicate changes to relevant team members
13. Address questions or concerns from affected employees
14. Confirm receipt and understanding from all parties

### Resources and Links:
- Current employee schedule master document
- Schedule change request forms
- HR policies on scheduling and notifications
- Employee handbook scheduling section
- Shared team calendar
- Communication templates for schedule changes

### Instructions:

Start by compiling a comprehensive list of all approved schedule changes including employee name, current schedule, new schedule, effective date, and reason for change. Verify that all changes have been properly approved through the appropriate channels (manager approval, HR review, employee agreement). For each employee affected by a schedule change, prepare a personalized written notification that includes: acknowledgment of their schedule change request or explanation of business-driven change, clear statement of current schedule with specific days and times, detailed new schedule with specific days and times, exact effective date when new schedule begins, reason for the change (employee request, business needs, project requirements, etc.), any impact on compensation or benefits if applicable, next steps or actions required from the employee, and contact information for questions. For schedule reductions or unfavorable changes, include supportive language and information about any available options or resources. Create or update visual schedule representations such as shift calendars, coverage charts, or team schedules showing the new arrangements. Before sending notifications, verify all information is accurate including dates, times, and names. Send individual notifications via appropriate channels (email, formal letter, or in-person meeting depending on significance of change and company culture). Document all communications in employee files for HR records and future reference. Update all centralized scheduling systems including shared calendars, time-tracking software, shift management tools, and any public-facing schedules. Notify managers, team leads, and colleagues who work closely with affected employees so they're aware of the changes. Be prepared to answer questions about the changes including clarifications on details, addressing concerns about impact, explaining the decision-making process, and discussing any flexibility or alternatives available. Follow up with each affected employee to confirm they understand their new schedule and have no outstanding questions. Set calendar reminders for the effective dates of changes to verify smooth transitions.

## Task 9: Issue New Job Sites Lines

### Steps:
1. Review current job site postings and active listings
2. Identify which positions need new or updated job posting lines
3. Gather requirements and specifications for each position
4. Research effective job posting language and keywords for each role
5. Draft compelling job titles that attract qualified candidates
6. Write clear, concise job description lines/bullet points
7. Include key responsibilities, qualifications, and benefits
8. Optimize language for searchability and applicant tracking systems (ATS)
9. Ensure compliance with employment regulations and non-discriminatory language
10. Review and refine all job posting content
11. Format job lines according to each platform's requirements
12. Get necessary approvals from hiring managers or HR
13. Post to designated job sites and platforms
14. Track posting performance and adjust as needed

### Resources and Links:
- Job site platform accounts (LinkedIn Jobs, Indeed, Glassdoor, etc.)
- Current job description repository
- ATS keyword optimization guides
- Employment law compliance checklist
- Competitive job posting examples
- Brand guidelines for job postings
- Hiring manager contact list

### Instructions:

Begin by identifying all open positions that need job site postings or updates to existing postings. For each position, gather complete information including job title, department, reporting structure, primary responsibilities, required qualifications, preferred qualifications, compensation range, benefits package, work location/remote options, and employment type (full-time, part-time, contract). Research current market standards for similar positions to ensure your job titles and descriptions are competitive and attractive. Look at successful postings from competitors and industry leaders for inspiration on structure and language. Draft attention-grabbing job titles that are both accurate and searchable - avoid internal jargon or overly creative titles that candidates won't search for. Create compelling opening lines that immediately communicate the role's value proposition and what makes this opportunity special. Write 5-7 concise bullet points for key responsibilities using action verbs and specific outcomes rather than vague duties (e.g., "Develop and execute social media strategy resulting in 50% engagement increase" rather than "Manage social media"). List must-have qualifications separately from nice-to-have qualifications to avoid deterring qualified candidates who might not meet every preferred criterion. Include information about company culture, growth opportunities, and unique benefits that differentiate your organization from competitors. Optimize content for applicant tracking systems (ATS) by incorporating relevant keywords naturally throughout the posting, using standard section headers, avoiding images or graphics with text, and using common job titles that candidates search for. Ensure all language is inclusive, non-discriminatory, and compliant with employment regulations (avoid age implications, gender-specific pronouns unless relevant to role, unnecessary physical requirements, etc.). Format job posting lines according to each platform's specific requirements - some sites have character limits, required fields, or formatting restrictions. Create multiple versions if posting across different platforms with varying requirements. Submit drafts to hiring managers and HR for review and approval, incorporating feedback and making necessary revisions. Once approved, post to all designated job sites including general platforms (Indeed, LinkedIn, Glassdoor), industry-specific job boards, company career page, and any relevant professional networks or communities. Track key metrics for each posting including views, applications, application quality, and source effectiveness. Be prepared to adjust posting language, titles, or platforms based on performance data to improve candidate quality and quantity. Set up alerts or tracking systems to monitor applications and ensure timely response to candidates.

## Task 10: Add Comments in Manager Streams Tracking Table

### Steps:
1. Access the manager streams tracking table/spreadsheet
2. Review recent manager streams and broadcasts
3. Watch or review recorded sessions for each manager
4. Evaluate manager performance across multiple dimensions
5. Assess content quality, delivery, and engagement techniques
6. Note specific strengths and areas for improvement
7. Document technical issues or production quality concerns
8. Analyze student/audience engagement and participation metrics
9. Identify best practices and successful approaches
10. Draft detailed, constructive comments for each manager
11. Include specific examples with timestamps when relevant
12. Add actionable recommendations for improvement
13. Note any training needs or support requirements
14. Record positive highlights and achievements
15. Update the tracking table with comprehensive comments
16. Ensure comments are professional, balanced, and helpful
17. Share feedback with managers individually if required
18. Track progress and improvements over time

### Resources and Links:
- Manager streams tracking spreadsheet/table
- Stream recording platform access
- Performance evaluation criteria and rubrics
- Previous feedback and comment history
- Training resources and best practice guides
- Quality standards documentation
- Manager contact information
- Feedback delivery guidelines

### Instructions:

Begin by accessing the centralized manager streams tracking table where all manager broadcast activities are logged. This table should include columns for manager name, stream date/time, topic covered, attendance numbers, engagement metrics, and your detailed comments section. Review the table to identify which manager streams need comment updates, prioritizing recent streams from the past week that haven't been documented yet. For each manager stream that needs review, watch the recorded session (if available) or gather information from live notes, attendance records, and engagement metrics. Create a standardized evaluation framework that assesses multiple dimensions of manager performance including: content quality and accuracy, presentation skills and delivery style, technical proficiency with streaming tools, ability to engage and interact with audience, time management and pacing, handling of questions and discussions, preparation and organization, visual aids and materials quality, energy and enthusiasm level, clarity of explanations, and professional demeanor. As you watch each stream, take detailed notes organized by these categories. Note specific timestamps for particularly strong moments or areas needing improvement (e.g., "Excellent explanation of concept at 15:30" or "Audio quality issues between 22:00-25:00"). Evaluate student/participant engagement by reviewing chat activity, questions asked, attendance duration, and any post-stream feedback or survey results. Identify patterns such as when audience engagement peaks or drops, which topics generate most interaction, and how effectively the manager responds to questions. Document technical issues comprehensively including audio/video quality problems, connectivity issues, screen sharing difficulties, platform glitches, or any technical interruptions that affected the stream quality. Note whether these were manager errors or system issues beyond their control. Recognize and document successful techniques and best practices demonstrated by each manager. These might include effective icebreakers, engaging teaching methods, creative use of polls or interactive features, excellent storytelling, real-world examples that resonate with audience, or innovative approaches to complex topics. Draft comments that are specific, constructive, and balanced. Avoid vague statements like "good job" or "needs improvement." Instead write specific observations such as: "Strong opening that immediately captured attention by relating topic to current industry trends. Consider adding more visual examples when explaining technical concepts as audience engagement dropped slightly during the theoretical section (35:00-42:00). Excellent handling of the challenging question at 1:05:00 - turned it into a great learning moment for everyone." Structure your comments to include: opening positive observation, specific areas of strength with examples, constructive suggestions for improvement with actionable steps, technical or logistical notes, overall assessment, and any support or resources that might help the manager improve. Maintain a professional, supportive tone even when addressing significant concerns. Frame feedback as opportunities for growth rather than criticism. Use language like "Consider exploring..." or "An opportunity to enhance..." rather than "You failed to..." or "This was wrong." For managers showing consistent excellence, provide specific praise and consider highlighting their streams as examples for others. Document innovative approaches or particularly effective segments that could be shared as best practices with the team. For managers struggling with specific aspects, note whether this is a one-time issue or recurring pattern. Identify whether they need additional training, technical support, mentoring, or resources. Be specific about support recommendations such as "Would benefit from training on interactive polling features" or "Might need upgraded microphone equipment for better audio quality." Ensure all comments are entered in the appropriate cells of the tracking table with consistent formatting. If the table has character limits, write full detailed comments in a separate document and include key summary points in the table with a reference to the full feedback document. Include quantitative data where available to support your qualitative observations: attendance numbers, average viewing duration, engagement rate (comments/questions per attendee), satisfaction scores if surveys were conducted, and comparison to previous streams to show improvement trends. Tag or flag streams that require immediate attention or follow-up action, such as urgent technical issues that need resolution, content concerns that need correction, or exceptional performance that should be recognized formally. If your organization has a formal feedback schedule, coordinate your comment additions with one-on-one feedback sessions with managers. Share your documented observations during these meetings, allowing for discussion and collaborative development of improvement plans. Maintain historical tracking of comments over time to identify patterns, track improvement trajectories, and provide evidence for performance evaluations. Regular review of past comments helps ensure feedback is having desired impact and managers are progressing. Set up a regular cadence for reviewing and commenting on manager streams - for example, all streams from the previous week should have detailed comments added by Monday of the following week. Consistency ensures timely feedback when experiences are still fresh. Consider creating a rating or scoring system alongside detailed comments to allow for quick visual assessment of manager performance trends over time. This could include numerical ratings for key categories (1-5 scale) plus your detailed written comments. Collaborate with other team members who may have observed the same streams to gather multiple perspectives before finalizing comments. Different observers may notice different strengths or concerns, creating more comprehensive feedback. Before finalizing all comments, review for tone and clarity. Ensure feedback is professional, constructive, specific, actionable, balanced (noting both strengths and growth areas), and free from personal bias or subjective judgments unrelated to performance. After adding comments to the tracking table, follow up with individual managers as appropriate based on your organization's feedback protocols. Some comments may be for record-keeping only, while significant concerns or exceptional performance may warrant direct conversation. Document any follow-up actions, training scheduled, or improvement plans in the tracking table alongside your comments to provide complete record of manager development journey.
